## Lecture 2 

- Capital markets: The fundamental mismatch - those who have capital are not the ones who need capital
	- What capital markets solve: Scale (individual, company, geography), efficiency (cheaper, less time), liquidity, price discovery
	- Core functions:
		- Capital allocation: Channel funds from surplus units to deficit units
		- Liquidity provision: Convert investments to cash quickly at fair prices
		- Price discovery: Aggregate dispersed information into market prices
		- Risk transfer: Enable hedging and diversification of other assets
		- Monitoring and Transparency: Market pressure on corporate management
- Who provides capital:
	- retail - traditional (401ks, etc.) + new app-enabled traders
	- institutions (heavyweights) - pension funds, insurance companies, mutual funds, etfs
	- alternates - hedge funds, private equity, venture capitalists
- Who needs capital:
	- Corporations (primary) - growth, r&d, working capital etc.
	-  Governments (largest)
	- Fin. Institutions
- Market Intermediaries - Investment banks, Asset Managers, Market makers, Rating Agencies
- Regulators (SEC, BaFin) - overseers, investor protection, market integrity
- Exchanges - organize trading, matching, listing standards, market surveillance
- Financial Products:
	- Equities - theoretically, a part of the company (common/preferred stock)
		- mutual funds, etfs, index funds for diversified exposure here
	- Fixed Income - interest
		- Government/Corporate/Municipal bonds
	- Derivatives - speculation
		- Futures, Options, Swaps (exg of cash flows; A **Swap** is a derivative contract where two parties agree to exchange future cash flows from two different financial instruments.)
	- Alternates
		- Real Estates (direct property or REITs), Commodities (physical goods like gold and resources like oil), Private Markets
	- Digital Assets (crypto, NFTs)
	- Structured Products
	- ...
	- Emerging Products: Sustainable Investing - ESG investments: Environmental, Social, Governance focus - screened funds

## Game-stop explained
The **GameStop case study** on this slide explains how a "Short Squeeze" happens when social media-driven retail buying collides with traditional institutional bets.
### GameStop Short Squeeze Flow-Diagram
1. **Traditional Setup (The Bet Against)**    
    - **The Logic**: Hedge funds like Melvin Capital used **Fundamental Analysis** to conclude GameStop was a failing business due to retail disruption and poor financials.        
    - **The Action**: They bet on the stock's decline by "shorting" it (selling borrowed shares) using options and future contracts.        
↓
2. **Retail Coordination (The Spark)**    
    - **The Tool**: Social media platforms, specifically **Reddit's WallStreetBets**, allowed individual investors to coordinate their buying power.        
    - **The Culture**: Traders adopted a "Diamond Hands" mindset (refusing to sell even when prices fluctuate) to outlast institutional risk managers.        
    - **The Access**: Apps like **Robinhood** and zero-commission trading made it easy for millions of people to participate simultaneously.      
↓
3. **Market Mechanics Breakdown (The Squeeze)**    
    - **Short Squeeze**: The massive surge in buying drove prices higher. Short-sellers were forced to buy back shares at high prices to close their positions, which pushed the price even further up.
    - **Margin Calls**: As hedge fund losses grew, their brokers issued "Margin Calls," forcing them to either deposit more cash or close their leveraged positions immediately.
    - **System Stress**: The sheer volume caused "Clearing and Settlement" stress, where the 2-day delay for trades to finalize (**T+2**) led to extreme collateral requirements for brokers like Robinhood.
---
### Key Terms Explained
- **Short Squeeze**: A feedback loop where a rising price forces short-sellers to buy back stock, which in turn drives the price even higher.
- **Margin Call**: A demand from a broker for an investor to add money to their account to cover potential losses on borrowed (leveraged) trades.
