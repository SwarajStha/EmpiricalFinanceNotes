- <mark style="background: #FFF3A3A6;">Seperation Theorem</mark> - indifference curve tangency to the cml line (slide 16)
	- Indifference curves show combinations of risk (volatility) and expected return that provide an investor with the same level of satisfaction.
	- **The Universal Portfolio**: According to the Separation Theorem, _all_ investors should hold the same risky portfolio—the **Tangency Portfolio**—regardless of their risk preference.
	- **The Personal Choice**: Indifference curves explain why, despite holding the same risky mix, investors end up at different spots on the red line. They simply adjust the **leverage** (the mix of risk-free cash vs. the Tangency Portfolio) to match where their personal utility curve hits the line
- Essentially, portfolio theory => All investors should hold the same 2 securities i.e. the tangency portfolio and the risk-free rate, and must choose the weight of each based on risk appetite

- <mark style="background: #ABF7F7A6;">Security **Marketline**</mark>: Replacing the tangency portfolio in the CML with the Market portfolio leads to the CAPM
  ![[Pasted image 20251105100430.png]]
	  - **r<sub>i​</sub>**: The expected return of the specific stock you are looking at.
	- **r<sub>f</sub>​**: The **Risk-Free Rate** (baseline return for zero risk).
	- **(R<sub>M​</sub>−r<sub>f</sub>​)**: The **Market Risk Premium**. This is the "extra" return the entire stock market earns over the risk-free rate.
	- **β<sub>i</sub>​ (Beta)**: This is the "volume knob" for risk. It measures how sensitive a specific stock is to market movements.
	    - **β=1.0**: The stock moves exactly with the market.
	    - **β>1.0**: The stock is aggressive (e.g., Tech). It swings more than the market.
	    - **β<1.0**: The stock is defensive (e.g., Utilities). It swings less than the market.
	    - **β=0.0**: Uncorrelated to market (only theoretical)
	- Example: If the risk-free rate is **3%**, the market return is **10%**, and a stock has a **Beta of 1.5**:
		1. **Market Premium**: 10%−3%=7% [(R<sub>M​</sub>−r<sub>f</sub>​)]
		2. **Stock's Extra Reward**: 1.5×7%=10.5% 
		3. **Total Fair Return**: 3%+10.5%=13.5% [r<sub>i​</sub>]
- Beta (B): Asset’s sensitivity to market factor
  ![[Pasted image 20251105100455.png]]
- ![[Pasted image 20260104173220.png]]
	- the vertical dashed line at **Beta = 1.0**. This marks the **Market Return** (RM​), which is the average return of the entire stock market.
	- the Slanted Line itself represents the **equilibrium** between risk and reward.
		- **The Slope:** The steepness of the line represents the **Market Risk Premium** (RM​−Rf​). A steeper slope means investors demand more return for every additional unit of risk.
		- **The Relationship:** It shows a **linear relationship**—as you move right (increasing Beta), you must move up (increasing Expected Return) to stay on the line.
	- #### Practical Use for Investors
	  Investors use this graph to determine if a stock is a "good deal":
		- **On the Line:** The stock is **fairly valued**. You are getting exactly the return the CAPM says you should for that level of risk.
		- **Above the Line:** The stock is **undervalued**. It offers a higher return than what its risk level (Beta) justifies. (overperforming market)
		- **Below the Line:** The stock is **overvalued**. You are taking on risk without being properly compensated by the expected return. (underperforming market)
	- Securities on the right of the market portfolio are more risky and the ones on the left are less risky
