- Order types
	- market - quickly as possible @asked/@bid-sell price
	- limit - when price hits certain asked price (not guaranteed to hit)
	- stop-order/stop-loss - turns into market order when certain price is hit (protect gain/limit loss)
- Bid-Ask spread
	- bid = highest buyers willing to pay; ask = lowest sellers accept => Ask - Bid = spread
	- large cap/liquid stocks have tighter spreads (lowers % of spreads)
	- spread is there because of exchange fees, tech infrastructure, holding costs for MM, etc.

- Trading Considerations (slide 26)
	- Liquidity measure
		- Bid-Ask Spread: The Direct Cost
		  The bid-ask spread is the most immediate way to see how "expensive" it is to trade a stock.
			- **Quoted Spread**: This is the basic gap between the highest price a buyer is willing to pay (the **Bid**) and the lowest price a seller is willing to accept (the **Ask**).
			- **Effective Spread**: This is the "real-world" version. It measures the actual cost of your trade compared to the midpoint between the bid and ask. If you buy at a price slightly higher than the midpoint, that extra cost is your effective spread.
		- Volume-Based Measures: The Activity Level
		  These measures look at how much "action" there is in a stock. Generally, more activity means better liquidity.
			- **Average Daily Volume**: The total number of shares traded in a day. Higher volume typically means you can enter or exit a position more easily.
			- **Turnover Ratio**: This is the daily volume divided by the total number of shares a company has (shares outstanding). It tells you what percentage of the company's ownership changes hands daily.
			- **Dollar Volume**: Calculated as (Price × Shares). This is the best way to compare liquidity across different stocks—for example, a $500 stock and a $5 stock.
		- Impact-Based Measures: The Price Sensitivity
		  This category measures how much your trade will "break" the price. If you try to buy $1 million of a stock, will the price stay the same or skyrocket?
			- **Amihud Illiquidity Ratio**: A famous formula that looks at the relationship between price movement (return) and dollar volume: Dollar Volumet​∣Returnt​∣​.
			- **Interpreting the Ratio**: A **higher ratio** indicates that a stock is **illiquid** because even a small amount of trading volume causes a large change in price.
			- **Practical Use**: This ratio is highly effective for comparing how "slippery" a stock's price is across different time periods or against other companies.
> 			**Why this matters:** If you build a strategy that looks great on paper (like the "Small-Cap" or "Value" strategies from the other slides), it might fail in real life if the stocks are so illiquid that the act of buying them drives the price up so high that your profit disappears.
