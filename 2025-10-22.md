- Sharpe ratio = risk adjusted returns
	- return per unit of risk
	- able to compare across different strategies and asset classes
	![[Pasted image 20260103150244.png]]
		- > _"For every 1% of 'danger' (volatility) I accept, how much 'bonus' return am I getting?"_
		 **High Sharpe Ratio:** You are getting a lot of return for very little "bouncing around." This is an efficient investment.
		 **Low Sharpe Ratio:** You are taking a lot of risk (a "bumpy ride") for a relatively small amount of extra profit.
		 - Imagine the **Risk-Free Rate (Rf​)** is **3%** (what you'd get from a safe government bond).
			The Scenario: Comparing Two Stock
			Metric **Stock A** (High Growth), **Stock B** (Steady Blue-Chip) 
			**Annual Return (R)** 15%, 10%
			**Volatility (σ)** 20%, 5%
			At first glance, **Stock A** looks better because it has a 15% return. But let's look at the "efficiency" using the Sharpe ratio:
				### Step 1: Calculate Excess Return (Re)
				We subtract the risk-free rate from the total return to find the "bonus" profit.
				- **Stock A:** 15%−3%=12%
				- **Stock B:** 10%−3%=7%
				### Step 2: Apply the Sharpe Ratio Formula
				Now we divide that bonus by the risk (volatility) to see the return per unit of stress.
				- **Stock A:** 12/20=0.60
				- **Stock B:** 7/5=1.40
- need to stress-test strategies and models as different time periods or geo-location can yield different results

## In quantitative finance, **Signal Construction** is the process of translating raw data (like earnings, prices, or company size) into a mathematical "trigger" or numerical score that determines whether a strategy should buy, sell, or hold an asset.
Based on the slides, Signal Construction serves as the "brain" of the strategy, converting a general investment idea into a precise, testable rule.
